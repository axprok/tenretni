The 39 Steps: The Novel and its Film Adaptations.

    John Buchan's (1875-1940) The 39 Steps: spy novel conventions (1915)
  • Paranoia and fear of war (WWI)
  • The action is here and now (not a historical novel)
  • Empire under threat (immune system works against the empire), only the hero can save it
  • Gentleman (the ruling class) hero restoring order
  • Villainous organization (agents as puppets not full humans)
  • Chance encounter (from the historical novel (remember Grinev meeting Pugachev in Captain's Daughter, George Washington in the Spy))
  • Chase
  • Riddle / McGuffin (Mr. Memory and the spy organization itself in The 39 Steps)
  • Technology (military secrets)
  • Woman as an obstacle (Pamela is initially a negative helper),
    The Thirty-Nine Steps film (dir. Alfred Hitchcock 1935), based on the 1915 novel by John Buchan (1915). The film establishes many features of Hitchcock's auteur style.
  • Chance encounter
  • Killing the protagonist in the middle
  • Humor: Saved by the sacred scriptures (joke)
  • McGuffin. The MacGuffin technique is common in films, especially thrillers. Usually, the MacGuffin is revealed in the first act, and thereafter declines in importance. It can reappear at the climax of the story but may actually be forgotten by the end of the story
  • Suspense
  • Icy Blonde
  • Technology as a visual attraction. In Hitchcock's film: aerogyro, trains, secret is silent airplane engine, the Forth Bridge in Scotland.
