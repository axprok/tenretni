Alfred Hitchcock

The 39 Steps, directed by Alfred Hitchcock, is significant for the development of the spy film genre because it established many of the narrative and stylistic conventions that later espionage films would adopt.

First, the film popularized the “ordinary man caught in espionage” plot. The protagonist, Richard Hannay, is not a professional spy but an ordinary individual who becomes entangled in an international spy conspiracy. This narrative device creates suspense because the hero must survive using intelligence and improvisation rather than training or official authority. Later spy and thriller films frequently used this structure.

Second, the film introduced the concept of the MacGuffin, a mysterious object or piece of information that drives the plot but is less important than the pursuit itself. In The 39 Steps, the secret concerns stolen military information connected to the spy network known as “the 39 Steps.” This storytelling device became central to many espionage thrillers and was later widely used by Hitchcock himself.

Third, the film established key elements of the cinematic spy thriller, including a secret organization of spies, coded information, a chase across multiple locations, and a protagonist pursued both by enemy agents and the police. The mixture of espionage intrigue with fast-paced pursuit scenes became a defining feature of the genre.

Finally, Hitchcock’s use of suspense, visual storytelling, and dramatic set pieces helped shape the style of the spy film. The combination of mystery, political conspiracy, and thrilling escapes influenced many later espionage films, including the structure later seen in works inspired by authors such as John le Carré and Ian Fleming.

For these reasons, The 39 Steps is often considered one of the foundational films of the modern spy thriller, helping define the genre’s narrative formula and cinematic style.
