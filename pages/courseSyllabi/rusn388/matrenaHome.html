Rusn 388 Matrena's Home.

Alexander Solzhenitsyn’s Matryona’s Home is a short story that demonstrates the incompatibility of the past and the present against the backdrop of rural Russia in the summer of 1953. The protagonist, Ignatich, aspires for a quiet, primitive corner of Russia in which we can teach mathematics after serving ten years in exile. He soon encounters Matryona Vasilyevna, an elderly “gipsy woman” who’s later slain by a product of human technological advance that she feared the most: a train. Solzhenitsyn uses a variety of literary elements to reach the conclusion that resisting human progress via primitivism is a hopeless endeavor (Solzhenitsyn 856-857, 865). Theme Solzhenitsyn explores human existence in Soviet Russia as a function of time. Matryona’s Home is set in “the summer of 1953,” but the location through which the past is explored, that being the time before the establishment of the USSR, is through the setting of “Torfoprodukt,” described by Solzhenitsyn as a place where the “trains don’t run” and where “trackless forests had once stood and were still standing after the revolution” (Solzhenitsyn 856-857). Therefore, the train can be said to be a time machine. However, Ignatich’s voluntary exile to a time and place largely outside of Soviet influence carries consequences: “On the train of Torfoprodukt…hung a stern notice, BOARD TRAINS ONLY FROM THE PASSENGERS’ HALL. A further message had been scratched on the boards with a nail, And Without Tickets.” It can be assumed from the intention of the transit authorities, likely influenced by state authorities against subversives and dissenters, that voluntary exile and isolation is permitted, but permanent: “Getting to Torfoprodukt was easy. But not getting away” (Solzhenitsyn 857). What can be insinuated from the train and its travel restrictions is that an antiquated world and the modern world cannot exist beside each other. Matryona and company would likely feel uncomfortable in a Soviet city or town just as much as Ignatich can’t stand the proliferation of them. This can also be insinuated in the physical distance between them, that being the need for a train to reach Torfoprodukt. There can’t be any chance for coexistence between the two ways of life underneath the hammer and sickle. Imagery Matryona’s house is a place where the ways of Old Russia are fading into extinction. When the narrator approaches the house for the first time, he notes that an attic window was “decorated in the old Russian style.” The shingles are rotting along with the beams and gates graying with age. The interior of the home is similarly dilapidated with the poor installation of wallpaper giving the room a “sort of inner skin” along with the presence of mice and cockroaches that fester within the dwelling. The pests, emblematic of the Soviet state, are corroding the last remnants of pre-Bolshevik Revolution life by their presence. The cat, old and limping, is emblematic of an incapacitated critic of the Soviet state: “The cat followed [the mice’s] scampering with angry eyes, but couldn’t get at them” (Solzhenitsyn 858-860). An object that is old may or may not retain the durability it previously possessed. In the case of Matryona’s house, the house’s collapse seems imminent given it’s structural condition, but it would be more appropriate to describe the destruction of her home through the dismantlement of her house’s top room by Kira and Faddey: “To get and keep a plot of land in Cherusti the young couple had to put up some sort of building. Matryona’s top room would do very well” (Solzhenitsyn 871). As they disassemble the top room, board by board, they’re also taking apart Matryona’s life in Torfoprodukt as well as Matryona’s identity as a rural Russian woman. Literary Form Solzhenitsyn used the literary form of a fiction short story to tell the story of Matryona’s Home. The reason why this was done was for practicality’s sake. Solzhenitsyn was inundated with the consequences of critical speech: [In February 1945] the military censor found passages in his letters to a friend that showed him to be…disrespectful of the Soviet dictator Joseph Stalin, and Solzhenitsyn was sentenced to eight years in the prison camps.” After his rehabilitation in 1957, he wrote Matryona’s Home among other works in secrecy before they were published in the literary journal “Novy Mir” (“The New World”) with the approval of Soviet premier Nikita Krushchev during De-Stalinzation (Puchner et al., 853-854). Solzhenitsyn’s decision to write Matryona’s Home as a fiction short story allowed him to arrange literary elements in any way he saw fit. If it was to be written as nonfiction, regardless of length, Solzhenitsyn would be bound to facts that couldn’t easily be manipulated to artistic liberty in the interest of authorial honesty. It would be a matter of fate as to whether or not certain literary explorations could be possible if done through nonfiction. The need to blend criticism of the Soviet state while telling a story that compels the reader to finish it afforded Solzhenitsyn some semblance of plausible deniability. That is, if ever questioned about the meaning of the story, more likely being accused of judging the Soviet Union, he could state that the meaning of the story deciphered by the accusative reader is his or her own conclusion that is neither correct nor wrong. Symbolism The train that delivers Ignatich to Torfuproduckt and ends Matryona’s life is symbolic of technological change. The only way that Ignatich could’ve reached such a remote region of Russia quickly was through the use of the train because of the land size of the country and the seeming encroachment of civilization, that being towns and cities, on the natural landscape: “I wanted to efface myself, to lose myself in deepest Russia…if it was still anywhere to be found” (Solzhenitsyn 856). The train behaves in a circular fashion in Matryona’s Home. First, it’s the means of escape from the modern world. However, the train also acts as a symbol of the modern world nullifying the old world when Matryona was tragically killed by the train: entrapment. Ignatich’s radio is a symbol of communication through advanced technological means. Matryona is unlikely to have had a radio before meeting Ignatich, but she’s intrigued by what the radio can deliver to her about the modern world beyond her house: “Matryona started listening more carefully to my radio.” However, she’s less than pleased with what modern Russia is doing in the 1950s. When she hears about the invention of a “new machine,” she’s upset: “’New ones all the time, nothing but new ones. People don’t want to work with old ones.’” A cloud seeding project is reported, she protests it: “’They’ll do away with one of the two-summer or winter” (Solzhenitsyn 867). When the radio plays the songs of her youth, she grows emotional over the sentimentality: “They were broadcasting some of Glinka’s songs. After half a dozen of these drawing room ballads, Matryona suddenly came from behind the screen clutching her apron, with a flush on her face and a film of tears over her dim eyes. ‘That’s our sort of singing,” she said in a whisper.” (Solzhenitsyn 867) This instance invokes the timeless power of art. Matryona feels relieved and comforted in the fact that time isn’t completely absorbed in technological advancements to achieve an economical end, such as inventions or cloud seeding. In this instance alone, the future has recreated the past to Matryona’s liking. Conclusion Solzhenitsyn’s employment of theme, imagery, literary form, and symbolism illustrated the hopelessness of Matryona’s elusion from the modern Soviet industrial juggernaut. No matter how much Matryona tried to extricate from the tracks of human progress, she would eventually crushed beneath them: “There was just a mess…no feet, only half a body, no left hand” (Solzhenitsyn 877).
