Master and Margarita. Modernist Narration

  • Modernist narration in Bulgakov’s novel is fragmented, unreliable, and self-reflexive.
  • Bulgakov’s narrative stance includes carnivalistic elements: characters-buffoons, characters-wise madmen, misalliance of the profane and the sacred (setting, characters, narration (Woland [Satan] narrates the Gospel), inversion of hierarchy (the novel opens with the “de-crowning of the king” (Berlioz, the head of Writers’ Union, Massolit, literally loses his head), transitional space (Apartment #50). Violence in Bulgakov’s writing is part of carnivalistic transgression.
  • Bulgakov’s narration combines grotesque buffoonery with philosophical parables about treason, moral choice, moral ambiguity, sin, and forgiveness.
  • His characters are often artists or writers. The writer (Master, Woland, Ivan) is a cultural hero who can be granted peace and forgiveness for his sins because of his talent. In his 1930s works, the major theme is the tragic fate of a writer-genius.
  • Female characters: Margarita is a misalliance character (a witch and a savior). See a similar character in Dostoevskii’s Crime and Punishment (Sonia is a saint and a harlot).
