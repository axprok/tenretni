Immitating the style of medieval chronicle.



The style of the Old Russian chronicle, especially the annalistic prose associated with texts like the Primary Chronicle.

A few features make this clear:

1. Anaphoric repetition ("More than one," in Russian: “не один…” (30)

More than one head rolled on the block [at the Red Square] ...
more than one train carried our foes ... beyond the Urals,
more than one red rooster crowed at dawn in a noble's mansion,
more than one general farted on the rack in the Secret Department ... (30)

"when" (51)) The repeated structure mimics the chronicle rhythmThis creates a sense of accumulation over time, as in year-by-year entries of the medieval chronicle.

2. Catalogue of punishments and events Chronicles often pile up episodes—executions, fires, punishments, omens—without deep psychological commentary. Sorokin exaggerates this into grotesque excess, but the base model is annalistic listing.

3. Moralized violence presented as historical record In medieval chronicles, brutality is often recorded tersely, as part of divine or political order. Sorokin mimics this tone—but pushes it into absurdity and obscenity, which is his postmodern twist.

4. Parataxis (loose, additive syntax) The long sentence strings clauses together without subordination—another hallmark of chronicle prose.
