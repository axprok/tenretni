    Why it is primarily modernist novel?
  • Focus on artistic consciousness The novel centers on the inner life of the writer Fyodor Godunov-Cherdyntsev and the process of artistic creation. This emphasis on subjective perception and the mind of the artist is characteristic of literary modernism.
  • Formal experimentation Nabokov plays with narrative form—especially in the famous Chapter 4, which becomes a mock biography of Nikolay Chernyshevsky. This structural experimentation and blending of genres align with modernist experimentation.
  • Intertextual engagement with literary tradition The novel constantly dialogues with earlier Russian literature—especially Alexander Pushkin and Nikolai Gogol—which is typical of modernism’s attempt to reinterpret the literary canon.
    Why some critics see proto-postmodern elements in the Gift
  • Metafiction The novel reflects on its own creation: by the end, the book Fyodor plans to write is essentially the very novel we have just read.
  • Playful irony and parody Nabokov parodies literary biography, criticism, and ideological writing—especially Soviet-style reverence for figures like Chernyshevsky.
