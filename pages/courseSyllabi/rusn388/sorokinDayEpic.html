Day of the Oprichnik. Sorokin's immitation of a medieval folk epic

In chapter 7, Sorokin imitates the diction, rhythm, and naming formulas of Russian byliny (oral epic songs), especially in their depiction of mythic monsters like Zmey Gorynych.

Here Sorokin shifts registers quite noticeably. This passage imitates the style of Russian epic folklore—specifically the bylina (былина) tradition.

    Key features that signal this:
  • Hyperbole is the key device: larger than live deeds and sizes. In Day of the Oprichnik, the oprichniks turn into a great dragon, fly to the US and kill and rape a lot of Americans.

    Sorokin mocks the epic genre by framing the epic deed of oprochniks as a drug-induced hallucination (77-83)
  • Formulaic repetition and parallelism: “А у каждой главы… / А перву главу… / А другу главу…” — this incremental, patterned buildup is characteristic of oral epic performance, where repetition aids memory and creates rhythm.
  • Mythological subject matter: The multi-headed serpent explicitly invokes Zmey Gorynych, a central antagonist in East Slavic epic tales.
  • Archaic numeration and forms: “перву,” “другу,” “третью-то” — these are stylized, pseudo-archaic grammatical forms typical of how byliny are edited or performed.
  • Epic naming formulas (“зовут-кличут”): Doubling verbs like “зовут-кличут” is a classic folkloric device, reinforcing oral cadence and ceremonial naming.
