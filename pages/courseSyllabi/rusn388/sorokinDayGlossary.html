Day of the Oprichnik. Discussion of archaic words and syntax

Тhe novel is set in 2028 (the future for the time when the novel was written: 2006) and Sorokin invents the language of the future when Russia turns into a medieval tsardom. Below are some words that signal this archaic language of the distopian "back to the future."

  • mobilov: cell phone

Immitating the style of medieval chronicle.

The style of the Old Russian chronicle, especially the annalistic prose associated with texts like the Primary Chronicle.

A few features make this clear:

1. Anaphoric repetition ("More than one," in Russian: “не один…”) The repeated structure mimics the chronicle rhythm—compare with formulas like “и бысть… и бысть…” (“and it came to pass…”). This creates a sense of accumulation over time, as in year-by-year записи.

2. Catalogue of punishments and events Chronicles often pile up episodes—executions, fires, punishments, omens—without deep psychological commentary. Sorokin exaggerates this into grotesque excess, but the base model is annalistic listing.

3. Archaic lexicon and institutions Words like воевода, дьяк, Тайный приказ evoke Muscovite-era bureaucratic and punitive structures, reinforcing the хроника + приказная (chancellery) style blend.

4. Moralized violence presented as historical record In medieval chronicles, brutality is often recorded tersely, as part of divine or political order. Sorokin mimics this tone—but pushes it into absurdity and obscenity, which is his postmodern twist.

5. Parataxis (loose, additive syntax) The long sentence strings clauses together without subordination—another hallmark of chronicle prose.
