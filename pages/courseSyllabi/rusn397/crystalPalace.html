In Dostoevsky's Notes from Undergound, The Crystal Palace represents a world built on:
perfect transparency
complete rationality of human behavior
mathematically calculable self-interest
a “final” social harmony in which all desires are satisfied by design

The Crystal Palace became a powerful cultural metaphor almost immediately after 1851. Russian radicals—especially Nikolai Chernyshevsky—adopted it as the emblem of a future rational utopia. In Chernyshevsky’s novel What Is to Be Done? (1863), the Crystal Palace appears as the architectural and ideological symbol of a socialist-futurist paradise, where rational egoism governs all human behavior.

Historically, the Crystal Palace was a real building constructed in London in 1851 for the Great Exhibition—the world’s first international exposition of industry, science, and modern technology.
