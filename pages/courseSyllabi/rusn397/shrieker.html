The Brothers Karamazov. Book 1 A Shrieking Disease (13-14)

In Literature and Culture Writers like Charles Dickens, Wilkie Collins, and Fyodor Dostoevsky
described “shriekers” as figures of madness or moral torment. The sound of shrieking symbolized: Inner torment made audible The loss of reason and social restraint The horror of confinement or guilt For example, in asylum scenes or prison passages, “shrieking” often marks the border between sanity and madness, or between the human and the dehumanized.
Modern Reinterpretation Today, we would understand those “shriekers” as people experiencing: Psychotic agitation Panic attacks Severe trauma reactions Neurological conditions causing vocal outbursts Their behavior, once pathologized and stigmatized as “shrill” or “mad,” is now seen as a symptom requiring compassionate, clinical response rather than a label of identity.
