rusn 397 Final Exam Study Guide

Part II: Essay Questions about Specific Films

  • Columbo — “Murder by the Book” (dir. Spielberg)
    • “Howcatchem” structure and parallels with Dostoevsky’s inverted detective plots
    • Doubling, split consciousness, moral ambivalence
    • Wealth and ambition as motives; reason vs. divine or moral intuition
    • Scandal-scene structure in TV detective storytelling
  • Taxi Driver (dir. Scorsese) and Notes from Underground
    • Physical complaints as existential metaphors
    • New York City as expression of psychic decay
    • Presence or absence of scandal-scene structure
    • Dream vs. reality
    • The trope of the hooker with a heart of gold
  • Kaurismäki’s Crime and Punishment (1983)
    • Revenge motive vs. “extraordinary man” theory
    • Investigator figure: questioning and justice
    • Absence of redemption; bleak ending; secular fatalism
  • Richard Ayoade’s The Double
    • “Lynchian nightmare”
      • Surreal lighting, claustrophobic sets, oppressive architecture, mechanical soundscapes
    • Dual performance of Eisenberg
      • Body language, speech speed, assertiveness, gaze
      • Identity, self-erasure, bureaucratic alienation
    • Genre
      • Dark comedy: ironized despair, critique of bureaucracy
      • Contrast with Dostoevsky’s Christian hope
  • Crime and Punishment in Suburbia (2000)
    • Gender inversion: Roseanne Skolnick / Raskolnikov
    • Does Roseanne have an “extraordinary man” theory?
    • Moral responsibility vs. environment
    • Abuse, dysfunction, suburbia as moral landscape
    • Violence and agency
      • Raskolnikov: premeditation, ideology
      • Roseanne: self-defense? retaliation? circumstantial?
      • Gendered power shaping agency
    • Does the film flatten or reinterpret Dostoevsky?
  • Robert Bresson’s Pickpocket
    • Key elements of style
      • Claustrophobic mise-en-scène
      • Physical action over psychology
      • Off-screen sound cues
    • Comparison with Crime and Punishment
      • Michel as modern Raskolnikov
      • Crime as compulsion from lack of love/faith
      • Alienation, pride, descent, redemption
      • No articulated “extraordinary man” theory
      • Where is faith? Bible? Who brings it? Who says the line about the “strange path”?
  • Akira Kurosawa’s The Idiot (1951)
    • Dostoevskian devices: doubling, distributed psychology, scandal scenes
    • Kameda, Taeko, Akama as Myshkin, Nastasia, Rogozhin types
    • Cinematic form: melodrama, staging, pacing, lighting, editing
    • Compassion and vulnerability in postwar Japan; resistance to violent masculinity
  • The Brothers Karamazov (1958 vs. 1969)
    • Historical & cultural context
      • 1958 Hollywood melodrama
      • 1969 Soviet psychological/philosophical fidelity
    • Genre & narrative changes
    • Cinematic style: mise-en-scène, lighting, sets, editing
    • Religious & philosophical themes
    • Trial scenes: framing, editing, psychology
