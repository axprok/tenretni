Gladkov's Cement. Complexity and difference from socialist realism

Ideological Ambiguity
Gladkov uses Marcus Aurelius as a point of contrast: is the revolutionary hero supposed to patiently endure suffering like a Stoic, or to violently reshape the world like a Bolshevik?

Intertextuality
Invoking Spinoza
Baruch Spinoza (17th-c. Dutch philosopher) stands for rationalism, clarity, and
an ethical system built not on dogma but on reason.
His Ethics emphasizes necessity: everything in nature follows from immutable laws, and
human freedom comes from understanding necessity.
Gladkov’s novel is obsessed with the clash between chaos and order — the chaotic aftermath of the Civil War versus the new “cement” of socialist construction.
Spinoza (professor Ivan invokes him at p. 182) is invoked as a thinker of rational order.
He represents the dream that history, politics, and
even personal passions can be understood and mastered through reason.

Ivan compares himself to blind Oedipus (183)
The Oedipus reference suggests: revolutionary certainty may conceal a deeper blindness to human costs.

Christian symbolism
Chapter 16 Tares (Chaff vs Wheat) (263-283)
The Soviet project of industrial and
social reconstruction is presented as a kind of “harvest.”
In this harvest, true grain represents loyal proletarians and revolutionary energies,
while плевелы represent remnants of the old world. (Cf. Matthew 13:24)
